Aussie Internet Blacklist Has Gray Areas

Vivian Wai-yin Kwok, 03.19.09, 03:50 AM EDT

The government's proposed filter, according to a leaked document, would censor a host of sites unrelated to terrorism or child pornography.


The Australian government is drafting law that would require Internet service providers to filter and remove all "inappropriate" content. Yet, the top-secret blacklist drawn up by the Australian Communications and Media Authority, which was recently leaked, included certain Wikipedia entries, some Christian sites, the Web site of a tour operator and even a Queensland dentist's practice.

The list of banned sites demonstrated that the scope of Australia's Internet censorship has developed far beyond its initial aim to block child pornography and Web pages associated with terrorism. There are about 2,395 sites on the list, which was obtained by Wikileaks, an anonymous document repository that publishes sensitive and secret documents leaked from government, corporations and religious organizations.

According to The Sydney Morning Herald, which gained access to the list, about half of the banned Web sites are not related to child pornography. These include online poker portals, YouTube links, gay and straight porn sites, euthanasia pages, sites belonging to fringe religions as well as some pertaining to Christianity and the home pages of private companies and medical practitioners.

Wikileaks, which has previously unveiled the blacklists of Thailand, Denmark and Norway, is planning to make the Australian list public.

Julian Assange, the founder of Wikileaks, told The Sydney Morning Herald that the Internet censorship regimes of various countries were "invariably corrupted"; as an example, he cited the more than 1,200 sites criticizing the Thai royal family that were included on Thailand's official list, which was originally set up to prevent child pornography.

The Australian Communications and Media Authority added Wikileaks itself to its blacklist on Monday. Anyone linking to Wikileaks on a Web site can be fined 11,000 Australian dollars ($7,424.40) a day.

In a report titled "Enemies of Internet Freedom" published last week by Reporters Without Borders, a Paris-based journalism advocacy organization, placed Australia on its "watch list," saying that the Internet restrictions could pose a threat to the country's democratic nature. According to the report, Australia is one of a handful of democracies employing a strict filtering policy. The Australian Communications and Media Authority was set up in 1999, with the power to regulate Internet content and to close down Web sites that are the subject of citizen complaints. The authority has pledged to expand the blacklist to comprise 10,000 or more sites.

Comment On This Story

The Australian Parliament in January 2008 examined a draft law requiring all Internet service providers to filter private connections to each home to remove all "inappropriate" content. The government justified the measure as an essential tool in the struggle against child pornography, as well as protecting authorship rights and guarding against defamation. Although the law is still in the planning stage, Internet service providers are required to carry out tests before the end of June 2009.

Reporters Without Borders criticized the Australian government for not making clear how the law would be applied: "The law does not say who would decide that websites were 'inappropriate'. It will not be users who will draw attention to content to be banned. This list is to remain secret. Neither does the law say how the lists will be drawn up of the websites to be blocked, or how a website could be taken off." The organization named Saudi Arabia, Burma, China, Cuba, Egypt, Iran, North Korea, Syria, Tunisia, Turkmenistan, Uzbekistan and Vietnam as 12 "Internet enemies."
